# Galileo mission at Jupiter: probes, moons, and radiation belts

The **Galileo** spacecraft orbited **Jupiter** between 1995 and 2003, providing the first long-term in situ study of the giant planet’s magnetosphere and satellites. A probe entered the atmosphere on a separate trajectory, measuring temperature, pressure, and composition down to extreme depths before link loss.

Galileo’s remote sensing instruments mapped **Jupiter**’s clouds at multiple wavelengths, tracking thunderstorms and zonal winds. Flybys of the Galilean moons revealed Europa’s induced magnetic signature consistent with a subsurface salty ocean, volcanism on Io, grooved terrain on Ganymede, and ancient cratered plains on Callisto. Gravity science measurements refined moon interior models and mass distributions.

Operating inside **Jupiter**’s intense radiation belts stressed electronics; repeated high-dose encounters eventually limited operations. Controllers executed a controlled plunge into **Jupiter** in 2003 to eliminate an uncontrolled collision risk with Europa. Galileo data remain foundational for comparing **Jupiter** system processes to those later observed at Saturn by Cassini and for designing radiation-hardened systems for future outer-planet missions.
