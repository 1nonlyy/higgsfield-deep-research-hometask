# James Webb Space Telescope: Carina Nebula cosmic cliffs imaging

The **James Webb Space Telescope** (JWST) observes primarily in the infrared, enabling penetration of dust that obscures visible-light views of star-forming regions. Early release observations of the Carina Nebula showcased “cosmic cliffs” where radiation and stellar winds sculpt interstellar material into dramatic pillars.

NIRCam’s wide field and high sensitivity resolved numerous embedded sources, including protostellar jets and knots previously unresolved by **Hubble** at comparable wavelengths. MIRI mid-infrared data revealed dust emission features diagnostic of silicates and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, constraining heating mechanisms in photodissociation regions.

Observers combine **JWST** imaging with ALMA molecular line maps and **Hubble** optical heritage to build multi-scale pictures of feedback in massive clusters. Public outreach color mappings are non-physical but pedagogically effective; quantitative science relies on calibrated flux units, exposure maps, and careful background subtraction near bright stars. Dither patterns and mosaicking strategies trade depth against areal coverage for crowded fields.

Archive pipelines release uncertainty maps alongside coadded images so users can propagate errors into photometric fitting. Community tools then cross-match catalogs to Gaia astrometry for foreground star rejection.
