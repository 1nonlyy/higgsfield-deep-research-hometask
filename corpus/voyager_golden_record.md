# The Voyager Golden Record: contents and fabrication notes

The **Voyager** spacecraft each carry a gold-plated copper phonograph cover and a 12-inch record intended as a time capsule of terrestrial sounds and images. The **Golden Record** was curated by a committee chaired by Carl Sagan and includes greetings in many languages, music selections, natural sounds, and encoded photographs.

Manufacturing took place under clean-room conditions to minimize Earth contaminants. The outer cover bears pictorial instructions for playback, including a stylus icon and a hydrogen spin-flip diagram meant as a fundamental timing reference. NASA documentation in this corpus describes material choices—uranium-238 plating for long-lived corrosion resistance and aluminum structural rings—but does **not** assign a public catalog number analogous to a consumer SKU. Internal logistics forms referenced only batch heat numbers for metallurgy QA.

Field teams occasionally receive questions about serial-like markings on museum replicas. Flight article handling logs emphasize that any stamped codes seen on public displays are exhibit identifiers, not necessarily transferred to the flight covers. For research purposes, treat the **Golden Record** as identified by mission and position (**Voyager 1** versus **Voyager 2**) rather than by a widely published factory serial. Public science summaries that discuss **Voyager 1** and the **heliopause** crossing sometimes mention the record in passing as cultural context, even though plasma instruments—not the record—provided the crossing evidence. The record’s scientific value for the mission remains symbolic; navigation and **heliopause** detection rely on radio tracking and onboard instruments, not on the record assembly metadata.
