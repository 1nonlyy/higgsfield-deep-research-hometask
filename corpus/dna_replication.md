# DNA replication in eukaryotes: origins, polymerases, and proofreading

Before a eukaryotic cell divides, it must duplicate its nuclear genome with high fidelity. **DNA replication** begins at many origins of replication scattered along each chromosome. Origin recognition proteins load helicases that unwind the double helix, creating replication forks that travel in opposite directions until they merge with neighboring forks.

DNA polymerases synthesize new strands using each parental strand as a template. Polymerase epsilon predominantly replicates the leading strand, while polymerase delta often extends lagging-strand Okazaki fragments initiated by primase-synthesized RNA primers. The sliding clamp PCNA keeps polymerases attached to DNA, increasing processivity. Because polymerases can misincorporate bases, intrinsic 3′→5′ exonuclease proofreading removes most errors at the moment they occur.

Additional mismatch repair pathways scan newly synthesized DNA for residual distortions. Telomeres present a special challenge: linear chromosome ends shorten unless telomerase extends repeat arrays in certain cell types. Together, origin licensing, fork stabilization, and layered repair explain how **DNA replication** maintains genomic integrity across billions of base pairs in complex genomes.
