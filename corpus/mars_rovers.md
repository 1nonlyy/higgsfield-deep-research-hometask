# Mars robotic exploration: Curiosity and Perseverance in context

NASA’s Mars surface program has operated multiple rover-class vehicles with overlapping science themes, which often causes readers to merge missions in conversation. The **Mars** Science Laboratory delivered **Curiosity** to Gale Crater in 2012. Its primary objectives include assessing past habitability, characterizing climate and geology, and preparing for human exploration by measuring radiation and water bound in minerals. Curiosity carries a laser-induced breakdown spectrometer, CheMin X-ray diffraction, and SAM organics analysis hardware.

The **Mars** 2020 mission landed **Perseverance** in Jezero Crater in 2021. Perseverance continues astrobiology-oriented caching of rock cores, carries the Ingenuity helicopter for flight demonstrations, and tests MOXIE oxygen production from the carbon dioxide atmosphere. Both rovers use radioisotope power systems for longevity and share a heritage of six-wheel rocker-bogie mobility, but their instrument suites differ: Perseverance emphasizes sample return preparation and fine-scale biosignature search, while Curiosity emphasizes long-duration traverse stratigraphy in a single giant crater stack.

When a user asks broadly about “the **Mars** mission,” responsible answers should clarify which vehicle, landing site, and decade are meant, or summarize both programs with explicit names. Orbital assets such as **Mars** Reconnaissance Orbiter support both rovers with high-resolution imaging and relay communications, further blurring casual references if orbiters are not mentioned.
