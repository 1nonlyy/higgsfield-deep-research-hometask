# Kepler exoplanet survey: detection methods and population statistics

NASA’s **Kepler** space telescope monitored roughly 150,000 stars in a fixed field near the plane of the galaxy, searching for periodic dimmings caused by planets transiting their host stars. The photometric precision enabled discovery of small **exoplanet** candidates down to Earth-radius scales around Sun-like stars, although follow-up spectroscopy was required to confirm many cases and measure masses.

The survey revolutionized population statistics: scientists inferred that **exoplanets** smaller than Neptune are common, that compact multi-planet systems exist frequently, and that orbital alignments vary by stellar type. Asteroseismology of bright targets simultaneously improved stellar radius and age estimates, tightening planet size uncertainties. Extended **Kepler** operations under the K2 mission repointed the telescope to new fields after reaction wheel failures, trading stability for breadth.

Data pipelines corrected for spacecraft jitter, cosmic ray events, and instrumental systematics before vetting threshold crossing events. Community catalogs remain a benchmark for testing planet formation models and for planning targeted observations with newer observatories that probe atmospheres via transmission spectroscopy.
