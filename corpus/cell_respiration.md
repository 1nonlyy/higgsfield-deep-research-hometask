# Cellular respiration: glycolysis, Krebs cycle, and electron transport

**Cellular respiration** oxidizes nutrient-derived molecules to regenerate ATP for cellular work. Glycolysis in the cytosol splits glucose into pyruvate while netting a small amount of ATP and NADH; under anaerobic conditions, fermentation pathways regenerate NAD+ from NADH to keep glycolysis running.

Aerobic organisms transport pyruvate into mitochondria, where it is decarboxylated to acetyl-CoA and fed into the **Krebs cycle** (citric acid cycle). The cycle releases CO2, generates GTP (or ATP equivalent), and deposits high-energy electrons onto NADH and FADH2 carriers. Those carriers donate electrons to the electron transport chain embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane.

Proton pumping establishes an electrochemical gradient that drives ATP synthase, molecular oxygen acting as the terminal electron acceptor at complex IV. Inhibitors such as cyanide block cytochrome oxidase, halting the chain. Together, glycolysis, the **Krebs cycle**, and oxidative phosphorylation explain how eukaryotic cells extract usable energy from glucose with high efficiency compared with fermentation alone.
