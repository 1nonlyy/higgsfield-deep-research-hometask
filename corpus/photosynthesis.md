# Photosynthesis: light-dependent reactions and chlorophyll

**Photosynthesis** converts electromagnetic energy from sunlight into chemical energy stored in ATP and NADPH, which later feed the Calvin cycle. The **light-dependent reactions** occur across the thylakoid membranes inside chloroplasts and rely on organized photosystems I and II.

**Chlorophyll** molecules embedded in those photosystems absorb photons preferentially in the blue and red portions of the spectrum; their conjugated porphyrin rings transfer excitation energy to reaction centers where charge separation drives electron transport. As electrons move along the cytochrome **b6f** complex and other carriers, a proton gradient builds, and ATP synthase uses that gradient to phosphorylate ADP. NADP+ is reduced to NADPH at the terminus of the linear electron flow pathway.

Water photolysis at photosystem II releases molecular oxygen as a byproduct—this is the source of atmospheric O2 tied to **photosynthesis** over geological time. Environmental factors such as light intensity, temperature, and CO2 availability modulate the overall rate, but **chlorophyll**’s central role in capturing light for the **light-dependent reactions** remains the defining feature of oxygenic **photosynthesis** in plants, algae, and cyanobacteria.
