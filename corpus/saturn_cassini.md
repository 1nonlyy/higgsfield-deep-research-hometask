# Cassini at Saturn: rings, Titan, and Enceladus plumes

The Cassini-Huygens mission orbited **Saturn** from 2004 through 2017, carrying a diverse European-built Huygens probe that landed on Titan in 2005. Cassini’s radar and infrared instruments mapped Titan’s methane lakes and dunes, while its ion and neutral mass spectrometer sampled upper-atmosphere chemistry.

The mission’s tour design exploited repeated flybys of icy moons, culminating in dramatic Enceladus encounters that detected water-rich **plumes** venting from the south polar fractures. Particle counters and dust analyzers characterized the plume composition, motivating ocean-world habitability discussions. Saturn’s ring system was probed at unprecedented resolution, revealing propeller structures, density waves driven by moon resonances, and the fine-scale dynamics of ring particles.

Cassini’s Grand Finale dipped between the planet and the inner D ring before atmospheric entry, measuring gravity and magnetic fields until signal loss. Archival data continue to support studies of **Saturn**’s seasonal storms, hexagonal polar jet features, and long-term magnetospheric variability. Lessons from power, thermal, and pointing margins inform designs for future outer solar system orbiters.
